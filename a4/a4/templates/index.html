Insights

I chose a line chart as the primary encoding because the data are monthly time-series observations with a natural temporal ordering. Position along the x-axis maps to date and position along the y-axis maps to the selected metric, which are the two most accurate perceptual channels for quantitative comparison (Cleveland and McGill, 1984). Each country is assigned a fixed colour from the Okabe-Ito palette so that colour meaning is consistent regardless of which country or metric is selected and remains distinguishable for viewers with the three most common forms of colour-vision deficiency.

Case counts across the five countries share a common baseline of relatively low values through 2020, but diverge considerably from 2021 onward. The United Kingdom stands out with the largest absolute spikes in the dataset: 6,112 new cases in November 2023, 4,408 in March 2023, and 3,688 in August 2023, well above any other country in a given month. The United States shows the next-highest peaks (2,380 in February 2024 and 1,791 in February 2023), followed by Canada (1,481 in April 2024). India and Japan remain comparatively flat in absolute terms throughout the period. Switching to the cases-per-million view reframes the picture: Canada reaches the highest per-capita peak at 38.99, with the United Kingdom second at 23.73, while India's per-capita figures stay below 1.0 owing to its population of over 1.4 billion.

The vaccination timeline tells a complementary story. All five countries show zero vaccinations through 2020, with rollout beginning in January 2021. In absolute terms India administers the largest monthly volumes by far (regularly exceeding one billion doses in a month), which is expected given its population size. The vaccinations-per-hundred metric normalises this: the United Kingdom reaches the highest per-capita figure in the dataset (177.15 in March 2024), with Canada close behind (144.82 in October 2024), indicating extensive booster uptake beyond initial two-dose coverage. The vaccination data does contain sporadic zero-valued months interspersed with high values, suggesting occasional reporting gaps rather than genuine pauses in administration; I retained these as-is rather than interpolating because the source of the gaps is ambiguous.

A few data points appear as sharp spikes well above the surrounding trend. These are flagged during cleaning (values beyond three interquartile ranges above Q3) but are retained rather than removed because they are plausible reporting artefacts: many jurisdictions periodically dump backlogs of previously unreported cases into a single monthly figure. Silently dropping these would misrepresent the source data; instead I keep them and note them here so the viewer can interpret the spikes in context.

It is worth emphasising that temporal co-occurrence of vaccination rollout and declining case counts does not, on its own, establish a causal relationship. Seasonality, behavioural changes, prior infection rates, and policy shifts all contribute to the observed trends. Correlation is not causation, and this dashboard is intended for exploratory visual analysis rather than causal inference.

Design Choices

Axis labels use plain English with units rather than column names from the CSV. The y-axis starts at zero to preserve proportional ink (Wilke, 2019). Top and right plot borders are removed to reduce chart junk. Gridlines are light and sit behind the data line. The layout uses a single-column structure that reflows at narrow viewport widths so the chart remains legible on mobile. Controls sit above the chart following the contiguity principle: the input and its effect are spatially adjacent, reducing cognitive load.
